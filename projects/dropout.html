Student Dropout and Graduation Prediction

A machine learning pipeline analyzing 3,300+ student records to predict graduation versus dropout outcomes, identifying the key financial and enrollment factors that drive student retention risk.

Methodology

How the Models Were Built

The project used Orange Data Mining to build a visual machine learning pipeline. Raw student data was loaded, features were selected, outliers removed, and missing values imputed before training.

Two model families were built and evaluated independently using separate data samplers with stratified 66/34 train/test splits. Each model was evaluated on accuracy, AUC, F1 score, precision, and recall.

Results

Key Findings

Students with tuition fees paid up to date showed overwhelmingly higher graduation rates, making financial status the strongest single predictor of outcome.
Scholarship holders were significantly more likely to graduate, reinforcing the protective effect of financial support on student retention.
Evening class students showed a slightly higher dropout tendency compared to daytime students, suggesting scheduling flexibility as a risk factor.
After age 35, dropout tendency increased noticeably, pointing to the added pressures faced by non-traditional students.
Recommendations

Institutional Implications

Based on these findings, institutions should proactively reach out to students with outstanding tuition fees early in the semester, expand scholarship availability to reduce financial dropout risk, implement dedicated support programs for evening and part-time students, and use predictive tools to flag at-risk students for early intervention.
